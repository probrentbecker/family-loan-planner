The tax side, in plain English

  1. It’s not a 1099 — and Bill doesn’t have to file anything for them. Banks send borrowers Form 1098; a private individual lender is not required to. The Lauers deduct the interest on Schedule A as “home mortgage interest not reported on Form 1098,” listing Bill’s name, address, and Social Security number. Bill reports the same interest as income on his Schedule B. The two numbers must match — a simple annual statement from Bill (this tool’s interest column) keeps everyone aligned.
  2. Only interest is deductible — never the full payment. Principal is not a deduction for them, and not income for Bill.
  3. The loan must be a real, recorded mortgage. For the Lauers to deduct anything, the debt must be secured by the home — a signed promissory note plus a mortgage recorded with the county recorder. An unsecured family IOU gets no deduction at all. Record it at closing.
  4. They only benefit if they itemize. First-year interest at 4% is about $8,300 — by itself well under the married-filing-jointly standard deduction. Combined with property tax and state income tax it might clear the bar; their tax preparer should run it both ways.
  5. 4% is below the IRS minimum rate for a 30-year family loan. The IRS floor (the long-term Applicable Federal Rate) is 4.87% for loans made in July 2026. Below that, the IRS treats the shortfall as if Bill received it anyway — taxable to him — and re-gifted it (roughly $1,800/yr at 4%; a paperwork nuisance, covered by the $19,000 annual gift exclusion). Cleanest fix: charge at least the AFR for the month of closing, e.g. 5%. If Bill wants them to net 4%, he can charge 5% and separately gift money back each year.

  1. A deduction never pays for a higher rate by itself. Every extra dollar of interest at 5% returns at most 22–24 cents in tax savings. Straight-up, 4% costs the Lauers about $35,000 less than 5% over 30 years even after taxes.
  2. But the Lauers probably get $0 tax savings either way. They only benefit by itemizing, and year-1 interest ($8,300–$10,400) plus typical Indiana property and state tax on a $210k home falls well short of the $32,200 standard deduction (2026, married filing jointly). Unless they have unusually large other deductions, the mortgage-interest deduction is worth nothing to them — so it should not drive the rate decision.
  3. The structure: a clean 5% note, with interest forgiveness as Bill’s annual dial. Carlie and Josh repay the full $210,000 of principal over time — that never changes. What Bill controls, one January at a time, is how much of that year’s interest he gifts back: $0 (he simply earns 5%), ~$1,500 (they net the original 4% idea), or all of it ($10,430 the first year, declining each year — see the interest column in the schedule above; always under his $19,000 annual gift exclusion), which makes the loan effectively interest-free for the kids. He can pick a different setting every year with zero paperwork changes — the signed documents are identical in every case.
  4. What the dial costs Bill. Whatever he gifts back, Bill still reports the full 5% interest as taxable income — that part is what keeps the IRS satisfied. So at the maximum setting, an effectively 0% loan costs him only the income tax on interest he then hands back (roughly $2,300–$3,600 a year depending on his bracket). That is the entire price of doing a 0% family loan legally.
  5. One rule: the gift must stay a gift. Separate check, never subtracted from a loan payment, and never promised in writing in the loan documents. If the note obligated Bill to give it back, the IRS could say the real rate was lower all along and the whole structure fails. Each January’s gift is Bill’s choice, made fresh — that’s what keeps it clean.
  6. The estate backstop, regardless of the dial. A 30-year note will likely outlive the lender, and nobody wants the kids making payments to Bill’s heirs. Bill’s will or trust should include a specific bequest forgiving whatever loan balance remains at his death. And if Bill ever decides he doesn’t want all $210,000 back, he can also forgive principal in any January (up to $38,000/yr on his own, $76,000 with his wife) — same rule, always discretionary, never promised.
